Methodology

Data Sources & Sample

World Values Survey (WVS) for religiosity data measuring importance of religion, service attendance, and prayer frequency.

UN Office on Drugs and Crime (UNODC) for sexual crime statistics per 100,000 people.

Sample: 13 countries with matching data including Australia, Germany, Mexico, and Myanmar.

Key Limitations

Small sample size: Only 13 countries with complete data available.

Underreporting: Sexual crimes often underreported, especially in conservative societies.

Correlation vs Causation: Analysis shows relationships, not causal effects.

How Variables are Defined

Religiosity Measurement

Index Scale: 0 (least religious) to 1 (most religious)

Three Components: Importance of religion, service attendance, and prayer frequency

Distribution: Most countries score between 0.55-0.75

Sexual Crime Definition

Measurement: UNODC rates per 100,000 people

High Crime Threshold: 75th percentile (675 cases per 100,000)

Caveat: Reporting and legal definitions vary by country

Key Analysis Questions

Question 1: Religiosity Patterns

Finding: Most countries show moderate to high religiosity (0.55-0.75 range)

Regional Patterns: Latin America and Asia score higher, Western Europe lower

Implication: Clear differences exist for comparison

Question 2: Crime Rate Variations

Finding: Wide variation in reported sexual crime rates across countries

Data-Driven Threshold: 675 cases per 100,000 as "high crime"

Challenge: Reporting gaps affect results

Question 3: Correlation Analysis

Key Finding: No clear relationship between religiosity and sexual crime

Pattern: Countries spread across all quadrants

Question 4: Country Profiles

Finding: All four combinations exist - no dominant pattern

Key Conclusions

Main Finding

The claim that more religious countries have more sexual crimes is not supported by the data.

Religious countries show both high and low crime rates, as do less religious countries.

Study Limitations

Small Sample: Only 13 countries with complete data

Underreporting: Systematic bias in religious/conservative settings

Complex Reality: Multiple factors beyond religion influence crime rates

Future Research

Broader Data: More countries and longer time periods needed

Additional Variables: Governance, culture, and institutions matter

Better Reporting: Standardized crime definitions and reporting methods
