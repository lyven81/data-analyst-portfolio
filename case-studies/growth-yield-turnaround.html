Growth, Yield, Turnaround

Each offers one thing, not all three

Three very different Malaysian businesses, read on the same seven-point yardstick: Empire Sushi, a fast-growing grab-and-go sushi chain; Magnum, a licensed 4D lottery operator in gentle decline; and MyNews, a thin-margin convenience-store chain investing to expand. Put side by side, they sort cleanly into a quality story, a value story, and a weakest-of-the-three, and no two of them are strong for the same reason. This report compares what the numbers say about each, and leaves the why to those who run the businesses.

What the data shows

1. Quality and price pull in opposite directions

Business quality vs the price paid for it
Business-quality score (out of 5) and price-to-book
Source: company 1Q/FY2026 interim reports; quality score on a seven-point yardstick.

On the quality scorecard, Empire Sushi is the strongest of the three: it is profitable, asset-backed and growing, scoring four of five. Magnum and MyNews each score three. But quality and price run opposite ways. Empire trades at about 4.8 times book and 24 times earnings, the most expensive of the three; Magnum at 0.7 times book and 10 times earnings, the cheapest. The market is already paying up for Empire's quality and marking down Magnum's decline.

2. One is growing, one is shrinking, one is standing still

Growth, and how much of each sale becomes profit
Revenue change and net margin
Source: company 1Q/FY2026 interim reports.

The three are at different points. Empire grew revenue 26% and keeps the fattest margin (15% of each sale reaches net profit). MyNews grew sales 12% but keeps almost nothing, its net margin is below 1%, so even a small cost slip nearly erases its profit. Magnum's revenue fell 11% as a lucky run of jackpot wins trimmed ticket sales, but it still keeps a healthy 8% net margin. Growth and margin do not travel together here.

3. The moats are not equal

The most durable advantage belongs to Magnum: it is one of only a handful of licensed number-forecast operators in Malaysia, a regulatory barrier no competitor can simply step over. Empire has a softer moat, scale and brand in a defined niche (grab-and-go sushi), with improving margins but no licence. MyNews has the weakest position: convenience retail is crowded and its sub-1% margin signals little pricing power. The cheapest business on the page, Magnum, also has the widest moat; the dearest, Empire, a narrower one.

4. Where the income is

For an income lens, Magnum stands apart: at about an 8% dividend yield, its cash generation funds a high payout that the other two cannot match. Empire paid out almost all of its profit before listing, but that was a one-off; MyNews pays a modest dividend while it spends on new stores. So the three offer three different propositions: Empire is growth, Magnum is yield, and MyNews is a turnaround-in-progress.

What the numbers reveal

Put side by side, the three sort cleanly and for different reasons. Empire is the highest-quality business but the most expensive, priced for its growth. Magnum is the cheapest and carries the widest moat and the best yield, but its sales are slipping. MyNews is the weakest of the three: it is growing, but on a margin so thin that quality and pricing power are hard to find, and its earnings multiple looks high because its latest profit was close to zero. No one of them is strong on every measure; each leads on one and lags on others.

The discipline this comparison rewards is matching the business to the question being asked. A buyer who wants quality and growth looks first at Empire and then at the price; one who wants value and income looks first at Magnum and then at the decline; and MyNews asks the hardest question of the three, whether a thin-margin retailer can grow its way to a margin worth owning. Which of those questions matters most is the part the numbers here cannot settle.

Method & data

Built from each company's most recent interim report (Empire Sushi FY2026, Magnum and MyNews 1Q/2Q FY2026), read against the prior year, and screened on a seven-point yardstick covering profitability, financial health, valuation, cash flow and dividend safety, business moat, management, and size. Prices are as supplied: Empire RM0.99, Magnum RM1.26, MyNews RM0.44. Valuation figures are provisional for Empire (recently listed) and read with care for MyNews (a seasonally depressed latest quarter). Figures in RM.
