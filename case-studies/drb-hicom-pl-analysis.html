Whose Profit Is It?

Group profit nearly tripled; owners kept a sliver

Data Stories LabAnalyst report1Q FY2026 · RM’000
Analyst ReportDashboard
⌂ Home

DRB-HICOM's group profit more than doubled in the first quarter of 2026, helped by a surge in Proton sales and costs that grew slower than revenue. It looks like a clean recovery. But two things sit under the headline: how the profit was made, and who actually receives it. This report lifts the lid on a sprawling conglomerate to show both, and leaves the why to those who run the business.

What the data shows

What we found

DRB-HICOM is one of Malaysia's largest conglomerates, spanning Proton and the wider automotive business, Pos Malaysia, Bank Muamalat, aerospace, vehicle services and property. In the quarter to 31 March 2026 group revenue grew 15.7%, to RM4.76 billion, and net profit grew far faster, up 141% to RM153.7 million.

Two threads run under that. The profit grew faster than sales because costs grew slower than revenue, a recovery driven mainly by Proton. And because so much of the group is owned jointly with others, most of that profit does not belong to the parent's own shareholders. The lid lifts on both below.

1. Profit grew far faster than sales

Operating leverage, working in the group's favour
Year-on-year change, by line
Source: DRB-HICOM Berhad, 1Q FY2026 interim report.

Revenue rose 15.7%, but profit from operations rose 94.7% and net profit 141%. When the bottom line rises much faster than the top, the cause is the cost base: it grew more slowly than sales. The group's combined cost of sales and operating expenses grew 13.6%, two points below the revenue rise, so more of each extra ringgit of sales dropped through to profit.

2. A wider margin on each ringgit

The operating margin widened
Margin at each level, this quarter vs a year ago
Source: DRB-HICOM Berhad, 1Q FY2026 interim report. The group reports cost of sales and operating expenses as one line, so there is no separate gross margin.

The operating margin widened from 3.3% to 5.6%, and the net margin from 1.5% to 3.2%, both roughly doubling. The group reports its cost of sales and running costs as a single line, so there is no gross margin to read here; the story is simply that the whole cost base grew slower than sales, lifting the margin at every level below it.

3. Where the profit came from

Cars and the bank carry the group
Pre-tax profit or loss by segment (RM millions)
Source: DRB-HICOM Berhad, 1Q FY2026 interim report, segmental note.

Split by business, the result is uneven. Mobility, three-quarters of group revenue and home to Proton (whose unit sales rose about 40% in the quarter), contributed RM180M of segment profit. Banking added RM79M. Against that, Postal lost RM10M and Services RM3M, both still working through their own difficulties. The group's profit is really the car and banking businesses carrying the rest.

4. From a ringgit of sales to net profit

Revenue to net profit bridge
How RM4.76bn of revenue becomes RM153.7M of net profit (RM’000)
Source: DRB-HICOM Berhad, 1Q FY2026 interim report.

The bridge shows how thin the overall margin is, even after the improvement: the combined cost base takes almost all of the RM4.76 billion of revenue, and finance costs take another large slice, before the contributions from joint ventures and associates nudge the result back up to RM192M of pre-tax profit and RM154M after tax.

5. Whose profit is it?

Most of the profit belongs to others
Group net profit split by who receives it (RM’000)
Source: DRB-HICOM Berhad, 1Q FY2026 interim report.

This is the part the headline hides. Of the RM153.7M group net profit, the parent's own shareholders received only RM45.0M, about 29%. Non-controlling interests, the outside shareholders of part-owned subsidiaries such as the bank and the postal business, took RM99.6M, about 65%, and holders of a perpetual sukuk took RM9.2M. So the per-share earnings the owners see, 2.33 sen, rose strongly too, but off a much smaller base than the group figure suggests. When a group owns big businesses jointly, the headline profit and the owners' profit are two different numbers.

What the numbers reveal

Put together, the quarter tells one clear factual story. Group profit nearly tripled because revenue grew while the cost base grew more slowly, an improvement led by Proton and supported by the bank, even as the postal and services arms kept losing money. But most of that profit belongs to the minority shareholders of the part-owned businesses, so the parent's own shareholders kept under a third of it. The recovery is real; the share of it that reaches the top is smaller than the headline implies.

One more thing keeps the quarter in context. The immediately preceding quarter showed a much larger pre-tax profit, but that figure was lifted by a one-off accounting gain of about RM334M on an aerospace acquisition; stripping it out, this quarter is an underlying improvement, not a fall. Whether the Proton-led momentum holds, and whether the loss-making arms turn, are the questions the numbers here cannot settle.

Questions the numbers raise
  • Is the Proton sales surge (units up about 40%, led by electric models) a durable shift, or a strong quarter that normalises?
  • Will the Postal and Services arms, still loss-making, turn under their transformation plans, or keep diluting the group result?
  • Given how much profit flows to minority shareholders, what reaches the parent's owners as the part-owned businesses grow?
Method & data

Built from DRB-HICOM Berhad's unaudited interim report for the first quarter ended 31 March 2026, read against the same quarter a year earlier. From the profit and loss statement it computes vertical analysis (each line as a share of revenue), horizontal analysis (the year-on-year change), the margin ladder, the revenue-to-net-profit bridge, and the split by segment and by who receives the profit. The group reports cost of sales and operating expenses as a single line, so there is no separate gross margin. The group also consolidates a bank (Bank Muamalat), so its balance sheet and cash flows mix industrial and banking activity. Figures in RM'000.
