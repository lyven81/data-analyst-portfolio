The Pass-Through

Why a utility's revenue growth isn't profit growth

Data Stories LabAnalyst report1Q FY2026 · RM million
Analyst ReportDashboard
⌂ Home

Tenaga Nasional sold 6.6% more electricity-and-services in the first quarter of 2026, helped by 7% higher demand. Yet its profit before tax barely moved. For a regulated utility that is not a disappointment; it is the design. Much of what looks like revenue is fuel cost passed straight through to and from customers, and the margin it keeps is set by a regulator. This report lifts the lid on a national utility to show why its top line and its profit move to different drums, and leaves the why to those who run the business.

What the data shows

What we found

Tenaga Nasional is Malaysia's national electricity utility, a regulated monopoly that generates, transmits, distributes and sells almost all the country's power. In the quarter to 31 March 2026 revenue rose 6.6%, to RM17.10 billion, on 7% higher electricity demand led by the commercial sector. But profit before tax rose just 0.7%, and profit after tax 4.4%.

That gap between a healthy top line and a flat profit is the whole story, and for this kind of business it is reassuring rather than alarming. Two features of a regulated utility explain it: a large part of the revenue is fuel cost that simply passes through, and the margin the utility keeps is set within a regulated band, so it barely moves whatever the sales figure does.

1. Revenue up, profit flat

The top line moved; the profit did not
Year-on-year change, by line
Source: Tenaga Nasional, 1Q FY2026 interim report.

Revenue rose 6.6% and operating profit a similar 5.6%, but profit before tax rose only 0.7%. The bottom line was held flat by two things below operating profit: a sharp drop in finance income (down RM139M from a year ago) against a large, steady finance cost. The operating business grew with demand; the financing lines kept the final profit level.

2. The pass-through

Fuel collected, then handed back
Automatic fuel adjustment over-recovery, returned to customers (RM million)
Source: Tenaga Nasional, 1Q FY2026 interim report.

This is the part the revenue figure hides. Under the regulated fuel mechanism, the utility passes the cost of fuel through to customers; when fuel costs less than the tariff assumed, the difference is returned. This quarter that over-recovery was RM862M, up from RM175M a year earlier, because fuel prices fell. So a chunk of the headline revenue was never the utility's to keep; it belonged to customers. Revenue growth that comes from fuel moving through the meter is not the same as the company earning more.

3. A regulated calm

The margin barely moved
Margin at each level, this quarter vs a year ago
Source: Tenaga Nasional, 1Q FY2026 interim report. The group reports operating expenses as one line, so there is no separate gross margin.

The operating margin held at 14.3%, against 14.5% a year ago, and the net margin at 6.4% against 6.5%. That stillness is the signature of a regulated business: the utility earns a return set within a band, so its margin does not swing with the sales figure the way an ordinary company's would. Demand and revenue can rise, and the margin simply stays where the regulator allows it.

4. From a ringgit of sales to net profit

Revenue to net profit bridge
How RM17.1bn of revenue becomes RM1.09bn of net profit (RM million)
Source: Tenaga Nasional, 1Q FY2026 interim report. Smaller income and cost lines are grouped.

The bridge shows where each ringgit goes. After the fuel pass-through and the operating costs of running the grid, RM2.45 billion of operating profit remains. Then comes the single largest deduction below it: finance costs of nearly RM1 billion. This is an infrastructure business built on borrowing, so interest takes roughly two-fifths of the operating profit before tax leaves the final RM1.09 billion.

5. One business, building hard

Almost all electricity
Share of revenue by type
Source: Tenaga Nasional, 1Q FY2026 interim report, revenue note.

The group is, in practice, a single business: about 96% of revenue is electricity, almost all of it in Malaysia. Behind the steady quarter sits a very large building programme: the company spent RM3 billion on plant in the quarter alone and has authorised about RM107 billion of capital expenditure over five years for grid modernisation and renewable energy. That spending is funded largely by debt, which is why the finance line looms so large, and why the steady regulated return matters: it is what services the borrowing.

What the numbers reveal

Put together, the quarter tells one clear factual story. The utility sold more power into a growing economy, but its profit was steady rather than rising, because a regulated business earns a set return and passes fuel costs through rather than marking them up. The margin barely moved, the pass-through returned RM862M to customers, and a near-RM1 billion finance cost, the price of a debt-funded grid, took the largest slice below operating profit.

The shape worth keeping in view is the trade the model makes: low volatility in exchange for low margin and heavy debt. A regulated utility will rarely surprise on the upside, but its earnings are unusually predictable, which is exactly what lets it carry the borrowing behind a RM134 billion asset base and a RM107 billion building plan. Whether the regulated return holds through the energy-transition spending, and how the rising debt is serviced, are the questions the numbers here cannot settle.

Questions the numbers raise
  • The fuel over-recovery returned to customers swung from RM175M to RM862M on falling fuel prices; how much does this pass-through move the headline revenue around from quarter to quarter, masking the steady underlying business?
  • Finance costs already take roughly two-fifths of operating profit; as the RM107 billion capital plan is funded with new debt, can the regulated return keep pace with the rising interest bill?
  • Operating profit grew 5.6% with demand; is that the dependable shape of future quarters, or will the energy-transition build weigh on returns before it lifts them?
Method & data

Built from Tenaga Nasional's unaudited interim report for the first quarter ended 31 March 2026, read against the same quarter a year earlier. From the income statement it computes vertical analysis (each line as a share of revenue), horizontal analysis (the year-on-year change), the margin ladder, the revenue-to-net-profit bridge, and the revenue mix by type. The group reports operating expenses as a single line, so there is no separate gross margin, and it operates within a single electricity business segment, so the mix shown is by revenue type, not profit. The automatic fuel adjustment (AFA/ICPT) over-recovery is a regulated pass-through returned to customers. Figures in RM million.
