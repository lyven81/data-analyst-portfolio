The Hedge Paradox

Profit jumped; the hedges lost RM511 million

Data Stories LabAnalyst report1Q FY2026 · RM’000
Analyst ReportDashboard
⌂ Home

Press Metal earned far more in the first quarter of 2026 than a year earlier, with profit up over 40% on barely higher sales, as the price of the aluminium it sells rose while the cost of the alumina it buys fell. But the same rising prices cost its hedges half a billion ringgit, so by one measure the quarter went backwards. This report lifts the lid on a commodity producer to show how both can be true at once, and leaves the why to those who run the business.

What the data shows

What we found

Press Metal Aluminium is Southeast Asia's largest aluminium smelter, an exporter that buys alumina, smelts it into aluminium, and sells most of it abroad. In the quarter to 31 March 2026 revenue rose only 5.2%, to RM4.10 billion, yet profit after tax rose 41.4%, to RM774 million. Profit grew eight times faster than sales.

The reason is a spread, not a volume story: the price of what it sells rose while the cost of what it buys fell, so the gap between the two widened sharply. But a second number runs the other way. The company hedges its selling prices, and when those prices rose the hedges lost money, RM511 million of it, which is why its broadest measure of income fell even as profit jumped.

1. Profit jumped on barely higher sales

Profit grew eight times faster than revenue
Year-on-year change, by line
Source: Press Metal Aluminium Holdings, 1Q FY2026 interim report.

Revenue rose just 5.2%, but profit from operations rose 73.7% and profit after tax 41.4%. When profit grows many times faster than sales, the cause is the margin, not the top line. Here the whole story sits in one line: operating expenses actually fell 4.5% even as revenue rose, because the main raw material, alumina, cost less.

2. The spread widened

Cost took a far smaller bite of each ringgit
Margin at each level, this quarter vs a year ago
Source: Press Metal Aluminium Holdings, 1Q FY2026 interim report. The group reports operating expenses as one line, so there is no separate gross margin.

Operating expenses fell from 87.4% of revenue to 79.4%, an eight-point drop, and the operating margin rose from 12.8% to 21.1%. For a commodity producer this is the heart of the business: it earns the spread between the price of the metal it sells and the cost of the inputs it buys, and this quarter that spread opened wide. The operating margin roughly doubled on a 5% rise in sales.

3. Where the profit is made

Smelting is the engine
Revenue and profit by segment (RM millions)
Source: Press Metal Aluminium Holdings, 1Q FY2026 interim report, segmental note.

Split by business, almost all the profit comes from one place. Smelting, the upstream business, is 85% of revenue and earned a 25% margin, accounting for essentially all of the group's operating profit. The downstream extrusion business earned a thin 1.9% margin, and the refinery and holding activities made small losses. The group's fortunes rise and fall with the smelting spread.

4. From a ringgit of sales to net profit

Revenue to net profit bridge
How RM4.10bn of revenue becomes RM774M of net profit (RM’000)
Source: Press Metal Aluminium Holdings, 1Q FY2026 interim report.

The bridge shows the widened spread reaching the bottom line: operating expenses still take the largest share, but a smaller one than before, leaving a much thicker band of operating profit. A contribution from associates, though much lower than a year ago, and modest finance costs and tax, bring the result to RM774M after tax.

5. The hedge paradox

Profit rose, but total income fell
Net profit versus total comprehensive income (RM’000)
Source: Press Metal Aluminium Holdings, 1Q FY2026 interim report.

This is the part the profit figure hides. To protect itself against falling prices, the company locks in future selling prices with commodity hedges. When prices instead rose this quarter, those hedges moved against it, losing RM511 million. That loss does not pass through the profit line; it sits in a separate part of equity. So while net profit rose 41%, the broadest measure, total comprehensive income, fell 56%, from RM566M to RM250M. The hedge that guards against a price fall is the same hedge that costs money when prices rise.

What the numbers reveal

Put together, the quarter tells one clear factual story with two faces. The profit jumped because the spread between the price of aluminium and the cost of alumina widened sharply, and that spread is earned almost entirely in the upstream smelting business. At the same time, the company's hedges lost RM511 million as those same prices rose, so its total comprehensive income fell even as profit climbed. Both are true, and both flow from the same move in prices.

The shape worth keeping in view is that this is a price-taker. Its profit is the spread between two commodity prices it does not set, and its hedges smooth that spread over time at the cost of looking out of step in any single quarter. A lower contribution from associates (down 78%) and heavy foreign-currency borrowing add further moving parts. Whether the wide spread holds, and how the hedges unwind as contracts mature, are the questions the numbers here cannot settle.

Questions the numbers raise
  • Is the wide aluminium-to-alumina spread that drove the profit a lasting shift, or a peak that narrows as input costs and metal prices re-converge?
  • The hedges lost RM511 million this quarter; as those contracts mature and the hedged sales are delivered, how does that loss unwind against the higher prices actually received?
  • The share of profit from associates fell 78%; is that a one-quarter dip or a lasting reduction in that contribution?
Method & data

Built from Press Metal Aluminium Holdings' unaudited interim report for the first quarter ended 31 March 2026, read against the same quarter a year earlier. From the income statement it computes vertical analysis (each line as a share of revenue), horizontal analysis (the year-on-year change), the margin ladder, the revenue-to-net-profit bridge, and the split by segment. The group reports operating expenses as a single line, so there is no separate gross margin. The hedge figures come from the statement of comprehensive income and the derivatives note; the hedge loss is recognised in equity, not in profit. Figures in RM'000.
