Mosques, Crime and Drugs

What Malaysia's districts reveal about religion and poverty

Data Stories LabAnalyst report149 districts · 16 states
Analyst ReportDashboardAI Assistant
⌂ Home

A common belief holds that more religious or conservative places have more social trouble: more crime, more drugs. Others hold the opposite, that faith keeps people in line. Most of the argument runs on anecdotes. Malaysia publishes enough open data to test it, so this report reads 149 districts and 16 states to see what the numbers actually say.

The answer splits in two. Areas with more mosques do not have more crime; they have less. But they do record more drug cases. And both results come down to the same fact: high-mosque areas are the poorer, rural, Malay-majority parts of the country. That, not faith, is what the numbers follow.

How religiosity was measured

Personal faith cannot be counted from the outside, so this report uses a physical stand-in: the number of mosques per 100,000 people in each district, from the national mosque register of 6,585 mosques. Denser mosque provision marks a larger, more settled religious community. The range is wide, from under 4 mosques per 100,000 in Kuala Lumpur to over 40 in Terengganu, so there is plenty to compare.

One caveat sits at the front: this measures the size of the religious community, which closely tracks being Malay-Muslim and rural. It is not a measure of how devout individuals are. That caveat turns out to be the whole story.

What the data shows

1. Do areas with more mosques have more crime?

No, the opposite. Across districts, more mosques per person goes with less recorded crime, a clear negative link (correlation of −0.26, statistically real). At state level the most religious states record about 113 crimes per 100,000 against 207 in the least religious ones.

Everyday crime is an urban, wealthier-state pattern. The highest recorded crime sits in Kuala Lumpur, Penang and Selangor, not in the religious rural belt, and the link holds after accounting for who lives there.

This is the reverse of the popular claim. On general crime, denser religious provision goes with a safer record, not a more dangerous one.

2. Do areas with more mosques have more drug cases?

Here, yes. More mosques per person goes with more recorded drug abuse, a strong positive link (correlation of +0.48). The most religious states carry almost double the drug rate of the least religious ones.

So the two outcomes point in opposite directions, and both are statistically solid. Anything that lumps crime and drugs together as one moral outcome is already wrong here.

The drug pattern maps onto Malaysia's poor rural east-coast belt, Kelantan, Terengganu and Pahang, and its long-standing trafficking geography, not onto personal devotion.

3. Is the drug link just poverty?

Partly, but not only. High-mosque districts are Malay-majority, younger and poorer, and each shapes drug figures on its own. Yet after holding ethnicity, age structure and income constant together, the drug link only falls from 0.48 to 0.36. It does not disappear.

This is the part a quick look would miss. Who lives in a district and how poor it is explains some of the drug pattern, but a real link remains once the usual explanations are removed.

It stays an area-level association, not proof of cause, and the arrow could run either way. Dense mosque provision marks the region carrying Malaysia's drug burden; it does not, on this evidence, create it.

4. Are religious areas poorer, or more unequal?

Poorer, but not more unequal. Households in the most religious states earn about half what those in the least religious states earn (RM 4,707 against RM 8,736), and their poverty rate is roughly six times higher. Across districts, more mosques goes with lower income.

Inequality is the exception, and it matters. The Gini score is almost identical in both groups, 0.35 against 0.36. The gap is about the level of income, not how evenly it is shared.

This is why the income control matters: when the drug link survives it, the finding survives the single largest difference between these places. Religious density, in this data, is a marker of the poorer, rural half of the country.

Quick answers
What is the crime rate in high-religiosity areas?
Lower. About 113 crimes per 100,000 against 207 in low-religiosity states. More mosques goes with less crime.
What is the drug rate in high-religiosity areas?
Higher. About 746 drug users and addicts per 100,000 against 379, roughly double.
What is the income in high-religiosity areas?
Lower. Median household income is RM 4,707 a month against RM 8,736, a little over half.
What is the poverty rate in high-religiosity areas?
Higher. About 9.9% against 1.5%, roughly six times as high.
What is the inequality in high-religiosity areas?
About the same. Gini is 0.35 against 0.36; the difference is in income level, not how evenly income is shared.
What about GDP?
We used household income as the measure of economic level, because reliable district GDP was not available across all states. On income, high-religiosity areas earn about half as much.
So does religion cause crime or drugs?
No. These are links between places, not proof of cause. More mosques marks poorer, rural areas, and it is that poverty and geography the crime and drug numbers follow.
Method & data

Religiosity is mosques per 100,000 from the national register, matched to districts by a name crosswalk that resolved all but one record. Drug figures are AADK's 2024 bulletin (users and addicts by administrative district, 2023). Crime is police-recorded violent and property offences by district, 2023. Population, ethnicity and age are the 2023 district series; income, poverty and inequality are the latest state series. Relationships are measured as correlations with significance tests, and confounders are removed by partial correlation, taking ethnicity, young-male share and income out of both variables. These are area-level links, so they describe places, not people, and cannot prove cause or direction. Recorded drug and crime counts also reflect how much a place reports and polices, not only underlying behaviour, so read every finding as association weighed against these limits.

Conclusion

The popular claim breaks in a specific, useful way. On crime it is backwards: more religious areas record less, not more. On drugs it looks right but for the wrong reason: the burden is real, yet it sits in the poor rural east coast and its trafficking geography, not in personal faith.

The one fact under both results is poverty. High mosque density and poor and rural describe almost the same places, and once you see that, neither result is really about religion. They are about where Malaysia's wealth, its cities, and its drug burden happen to sit.

For anyone weighing this claim in future, an area-level link between religion and vice is almost always standing in for income and geography. Ask what else is true of those places before you credit the faith.
