Good and Fairly Priced

Only one of three giants is both

Data Stories LabComparative analysis1Q FY2026 · RM
Analyst ReportDashboard
⌂ Home

Three of Bursa Malaysia's largest companies, read on the same seven-point yardstick: DRB-HICOM, a sprawling conglomerate; Press Metal, the region's largest aluminium smelter; and Tenaga Nasional, the national electricity utility. They span the full range of the trade every investor makes, between how good a business is and how much its shares cost. One is the cheapest and the weakest, one is the best and the dearest, and one sits in the middle on both. This report compares what the numbers say about each, and leaves the why to those who run the businesses.

What the data shows

1. Quality and price, neatly inverted

The better the business, the higher the price
Business-quality score (out of 5) and price-to-book
Source: company 1Q FY2026 interim reports; quality score on a seven-point yardstick.

The three line up almost perfectly along the quality-price trade. Press Metal scores a clean five of five, the strongest business of the set, and the market charges for it: nearly seven times book. DRB-HICOM scores three, the weakest, and trades at about a quarter of book, the deepest discount. Tenaga sits between them on both, four of five at about 1.7 times book. You can buy the best business or the cheapest one, but not the same one.

2. The returns tell the same story from a different angle

How hard each business works its equity
Return on equity, and net-profit growth this quarter
Source: company 1Q FY2026 interim reports.

Press Metal earns about 27% on equity, a genuinely high return that explains its premium. Tenaga earns a steady ~9%, the modest but dependable return of a regulated utility. DRB-HICOM earns only about 2% at the owner level, because so much of its profit flows to the minority shareholders of its part-owned banking and postal businesses, which is why it trades so cheaply. High profit growth this quarter (DRB's +141%) is not the same as a high return on the capital employed.

3. Three different propositions

Each giant offers a distinct shape. DRB-HICOM is a deep-value, sum-of-parts bet: cheap on assets, with real national franchises (Proton, a bank, the postal service), but low returns and a complex, heavily minority-owned structure. Press Metal is a high-quality grower: a low-cost smelter compounding at a high return, but its earnings are commodity-cyclical and its price already assumes the quality continues. Tenaga is a defensive income holding: a regulated monopoly with predictable earnings and a ~3.5% yield, fairly rather than cheaply priced.

4. Only one is both good and fairly priced

Read across the scorecard, Tenaga is the one company here that clears the bar on both halves of the trade: a strong, wide-moat business and a fair, not stretched, valuation. Press Metal is the higher-quality business, but the price already reflects it; DRB-HICOM is the cheaper share, but the business and the owner-level returns are weaker. The exercise is a reminder that the strongest screen result is not the best business or the cheapest share on its own, but the place where good quality and a sensible price meet.

What the numbers reveal

Put side by side, the three map the whole spectrum of the quality-price trade. Press Metal is the best business and the dearest; DRB-HICOM is the cheapest and the weakest, its low price the market's verdict on low owner-level returns and complexity; and Tenaga sits in the middle on quality and price, the only one that is both a strong business and a reasonable valuation at once. Each is large, durable and well-run in its own way; they differ in what an owner pays for that, and in what return the equity earns.

The discipline this comparison rewards is separating the two questions a price contains: how good is the business, and how much am I paying for it. Press Metal answers the first question best and the second worst; DRB-HICOM the reverse; Tenaga answers both adequately. Whether to pay up for the best, wait for the dearest to cheapen, or own the steady middle is the part the numbers here cannot settle.

Method & data

Built from each company's 1Q FY2026 interim report, read against the same quarter a year earlier, and screened on a seven-point yardstick covering profitability, financial health, valuation, cash flow and dividend safety, business moat, management, and size. Prices are as supplied: DRB-HICOM RM1.03, Press Metal RM7.70, Tenaga RM14.34. Two of the three carry features that the standard ratios read with care: DRB-HICOM consolidates a bank and is heavily minority-owned, and Tenaga is a regulated utility whose revenue includes a fuel pass-through.
